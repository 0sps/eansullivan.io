Nanotechnology Project

For an entire semester, I worked alongside three engineering students to complete
two projects.

The first was a microfluidics project studying cell adhesion of yeast. Specifically, our task was to determine the effect that a treatment would have in eliminating yeast buildup
in a microchannel. My group created and designed a microchip with multiple channels
that supported such fermentation, and tested the ability of saline (in varying concentrations) to shear the yeast off the microchannel walls. Due to unforeseen circumstances, the experiment was not able to be fully conducted, however all research and design work can be found in the Microfluidics section of the website.

The second project involved creating a lab-on-a-chip capable of detecting a specific disease using only a drop of blood. The team decided to pursue Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) after considering multiple other diseases such as ovarian cancer and tuberculosis. The lab-on-a-chip designed by the team is capable of taking a drop of blood,
filtering it to receive plasma, and then detecting the presence of the p24 protein, a protein specific to HIV. This project culminated in an NIH grant proposal and poster presentation. All information regarding this project can be found in the Nanotechnology section of the website, including the various design stages, presentations, and grant proposal.


Microfluidics Project
Annotated Bibliography

Before any designs were created, each team member conducted initial research on articles they found interesting. Each team member created an annotated bibliography to assist them in the research for the microfluidics project. The goal of doing this work before the main project was so that each team member had a base of preexisting knowledge in the world of microfluidics, and they could easily reference and cite a source from their annotated bibliography if needed.
