For an entire semester, I worked alongside three engineering students to complete
two projects.

The first was a microfluidics project studying cell adhesion of yeast. Specifically, our task was to determine the effect that a treatment would have in eliminating yeast buildup
in a microchannel. My group created and designed a microchip with multiple channels
that supported such fermentation, and tested the ability of saline (in varying concentrations) to shear the yeast off the microchannel walls. Due to unforeseen circumstances, the experiment was not able to be fully conducted, however all research and design work can be found in the Microfluidics section of the website.

The second project involved creating a lab-on-a-chip capable of detecting a specific disease using only a drop of blood. The team decided to pursue Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) after considering multiple other diseases such as ovarian cancer and tuberculosis. The lab-on-a-chip designed by the team is capable of taking a drop of blood,
filtering it to receive plasma, and then detecting the presence of the p24 protein, a protein specific to HIV. This project culminated in an NIH grant proposal and poster presentation. All information regarding this project can be found in the Nanotechnology section of the website, including the various design stages, presentations, and grant proposal.

Design Stage 2
In design stage 2, a preliminary design for the microchip was decided. CAD drawings were produced for this design, as shown below. The team settled on a square for the bottom and top of the chip holder, with a round PMMA chip inside. The chip consists of four microchannels, each connected by a round inlet or outlet area on the end. Through preliminary research, the team determined that a wider entrance would allow for greater efficiency in achieving flow, and would reduce the shear stress imparted on the very first sections of the microchannel walls. The chip, therefore, has a wider inlet opening.

CAD Chip Design

Embedded below are the CAD drawings for our chip. The chip holders are square for easier manufacturing, and also have input and output slots instead of individual holes for each channel. These slots make insertion of the fluid when performing washes much easier. The chip itself has a top with 4  equal dimension channels, and a bottom to cover them. The 4 channels were made equal dimensions so that all four channels could be seeded with yeast simultaneously. This makes the collection of data much more efficient.

Experimental Procedure

As part of the microfluidics project, my team created a comprehensive experimental procedure for testing a microchip.

The procedure aims to test the shearing capability of saline when added to microchannels with bioadhered yeast.

First, it involves incubating yeast for several minutes, first outside and then inside the microchannel. It then requires a low-pressure flush, a gentle and slow movement of water through the microchannel, to remove
any yeast cells that did not bond to the biofilm during the incubation time. Then, a solution of water or one of three concentrations of saline
will be run through the chip for one minute. Afterward, the amount of remaining yeast cells in the microchip was analyzed using a microscope.
This process was repeated at different fluid velocities, so as to test the additional affect velocity may have on yeast cell adhesion.
Through this procedure, we sought to determine whether the salt content of a saline solution could cause more yeast cells to shear and fall out
of the microchannel.

Experimental Statistics

My group worked extensively on experimental statistics knowledge as part of the microfluidics experiment.

Each team member individually completed an assignment tailored to statistics, thereby learning terminology and procedures associated with
statistical analysis. My team learned how to set up an experiment by creating null and alternative hypothesis, as well as methods to test
them using t-tests. We used this knowledge in the microfluidics experiment, where we determined null and alternative hypotheses, as well as criteria for rejecting their preconceptions. We were required to interpret different scenarios and determine null and alternative hypothesis, as
well as interpret results from a t-test and determine whether they constituted a rejection of the null.
